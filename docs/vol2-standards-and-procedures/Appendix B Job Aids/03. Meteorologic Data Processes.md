# Job Aid 3—Meteorologic Data Processes

This job aid provides supplemental information and details related to the production of meteorologic data for an FFRD study.

## Watershed Boundary Input

The watershed boundary for an FFRD study is clearly defined during the scoping phase. This boundary is provided as input to the meteorology Project Delivery Team member for determining the appropriate transposition domain and assembling the collection of storms for the study.

The watershed domain as a geopackage with a single part polygon is stored in the root.

## Transposition Region Selection

Storms do not occur consistently throughout the United States. To ensure storms are only moved within areas where the storm is equally probable to exist, the areas for collecting storms to transpose are limited to a transposition domain. A transposition domain represents a spatial area having homogeneous meteorologic conditions. Developing a good transposition domain requires meteorologic training. Transposition domains are generated by a small team and are delivered to the shared *basin-data* folder of the project directory.

A transposition region is the key assumption and driving factor in the Stochastic Storm Transposition (SST) approach for generating synthetic precipitation events. This domain is a geographic extent that experiences homogeneous storm-generating meteorology and contains the study watershed. The foundational assumption of SST is that a storm that occurred at one location within the transposition domain can freely be transposed to any other location within the domain. The size and shape of the transposition domain extent is informed by meteorological processes.

Rough initial domains can be established using products such as the U.S. Climate Divisions by starting with the division(s) that contain the study watershed and adding adjacent and nearby divisions that are sufficiently similar based on climate. The divisions have several limitations that make them unfit for generating a final transposition domain but can be indicative of transitions between climatic zones within a state. Comparing these boundaries to regions of high gradient in climatic variables (e.g., annual precipitation) or physical characteristics (e.g., elevation) can indicate meteorological driving mechanisms in the region.

An initial investigation of historical flood and storm causal mechanisms for the study watershed is warranted. Storm and flood reports from the largest events on record provide narrative descriptions and data summaries for significant events and an impression of important storms in the region. A transposition domain should contain the area around the study watershed where these kinds of storms are also likely and does not contain physical features that would prevent or drastically alter the behavior of a storm in that area (e.g., mountain ranges).

An initial screening step should be an evaluation of tropical cyclone (TC) activity using a dataset such as [International Best Track Archive for Climate Stewardship](https://www.ncei.noaa.gov/products/international-best-track-archive) (IBTrACS). TC activity can be visualized relative to the study watershed and areas with similar numbers of storm tracks are likely to be included in the transposition domain. The tracks may also reflect important physical features, such as blocking mountain ranges, that would need to be excluded. In areas where few TCs affect the watershed, areas of high TC activity should be excluded from the domain. Coastally adjacent areas are likely to have many TC tracks, and the domain should contain similar levels of activity throughout. An important boundary delineator in this case is often the distance from the coastline.

A meteorologist will take the input Hydrologic Unit Code (HUC)-4 watershed domain and utilize key datasets to generate a transposition domain.

Promising techniques that utilize extremal statistics of rainfall maxima determine transposition regions directly based on observed data may also be considered.

The transposition region for the study is developed during the Scoping and Data Preparation phase, stored as a geopackage with a single part polygon and posted to the location below.

<u>Delivery Location</u>: basin-name \> basin-data \> storm-catalog \> transposition-region

## Storm Catalog Development

Storms are identified from a source of gridded precipitation that has a long period of record. Currently the appropriate data source is the Analysis of Record for Calibration (AORC) dataset. It has 45 years of record with hourly precipitation grids, at 4- or 1-kilometer resolution.

Storms are selected based on the target watershed; the objective is to find a storm that optimizes the precipitation over the target watershed for a given duration.

A threshold is leveraged to select storms that exceed a cumulative precipitation total, which helps to define which storms are included or excluded from the catalog. The threshold is set to target a number of targeted storms per year over the duration of the input datasource. For instance, in a 45-year data source, if the objective count of storms per year is 10, a threshold is set to collect 450 storms from the datasource that optimize the storm over the size of the target watershed.

The following inputs are required:

1.  A datasource of gridded precipitation (AORC dataset used for FFRD studies)

2.  A transposition domain (defined according to guidelines and considerations above)

3.  A watershed domain (typically HUC-4 watersheds)

4.  A user defined storm duration (72 hours for FFRD studies)

5.  A target threshold (average of 10 storms per year for FFRD studies)

### Storm Catalog Development Procedure

The following algorithmic procedures are employed by the cloud-compute, storm-cloud plugin to develop a ranking of storm events within the transposition region, and build the storm catalog for use within the SST framework.

Storm events were identified from the aggregated AORC data by looking at rolling 72-hour precipitation totals and finding events with significant 72-hour precipitation over an area the size and shape of the target watershed. Beginning at *t*=0, which corresponds to the first day of the period of record (01 January 1979), the procedure works as follows:

1.  Aggregate the daily product to a three-day total by taking the sum of the three daily total precipitation rasters (*t*, *t*+1, and *t*+2).

<!-- -->

1.  Optimize the position of the watershed polygon within the transposition domain such that the three-day precipitation is maximized when averaged across the polygon. Limit the movement of the watershed polygon so no portion is ever outside the transposition domain during the search for the optimum position.

2.  Compute summary statistics for the precipitation inside the optimized watershed polygon position and associate those with the date *t.*

3.  Increment *t* by 1 and return to Step 1.

This process results in a list of dates with associated precipitation depths creating severe storms in an area the size and shape of the target watershed. The storm dates are separated such that the largest precipitation in each 72-hour window is retained, ensuring the same storm is not double counted. Once there is a complete listing of event dates and the associated maximized watershed precipitation, a threshold is chosen to isolate the most severe events. A threshold that results in an average of *k* events per year is determined to select storms. The threshold is applied to all storms and the *k*×number of years largest storms in the storm datasource are selected and added to the catalog.

For each storm event selected to be included in the catalog, additional metadata beyond the rainfall fields are computed and retained.

- The location of the storm center is stored as a coordinate pair, determined by the center of the pixel where the maximum total accumulation of rainfall occurred.

- The beginning date of the storm event.

- The minimum, average, maximum, and total values of the rainfall accumulation pixel values in the maximized position over the watershed.

- The storm type (assignment of type described in separate section)

- The ratio of the mean accumulation in the maximized position to the average of the 72-hour 2-year rainfall from NOAA Atlas 14 at the same location.

No assumption is made about whether an individual event is an annual maximum with respect to any statistic, and creating an annual maximum series is not necessary. When the transpositions are performed, the storms themselves are never treated as annual maxima, only one of many possible realizations of a storm event within a year.

### Delivery of Storm Catalog

The selected storm catalog storms are delivered in named Data Storage System (DSS) files as well as in the form of a full catalog in HEC-HMS .grid format. This allows for easy importing into HEC-HMS for evaluation.

In a sub directory called, *seasonality-distributions*, a .csv of the cumulative density function of the day of year with the probability of the storm of a given storm type occurring on that day is stored and named by storm type. In a sub directory called, *storms*, the .dss files containing precipitation and temperature are stored.

## Normalization Grid for Transposition Region

A normalization grid is created to support the Normalized Transposition technique to improve the performance of SST. Section 5.7 of SOP Volume II includes the motivation and recommendations for developing and applying a normalization grid as part of the SST procedure. In areas where NOAA Atlas 14 is available and fully covers the transposition domain, the annual maximum series-derived, 72-hour, 100-year precipitation field should be used to normalize. It may be necessary to mosaic together several rasters spanning multiple volumes of Atlas 14. The rasters should be generated in .GeoTIFF format. When Atlas 15 becomes available, it should replace any usages of Atlas 14. It is anticipated that Atlas 15 will provide a seamless product that is spatially consistent and will not require creating a mosaic.

Where Atlas 14 or 15 is not available, the preferred normalization field is a 72-hour mean annual maximum field developed from the source precipitation dataset (AORC or similar). Parameter-elevation Regressions on Independent Slopes Model (PRISM) normal or seasonal precipitation should not be used.

<u>Delivery Location</u>: basin-name \> basin-data \> storm-catalog \> normalization-grid

## Storm Typing

Storm typing is a novel addition to the traditional SST recipe that improves the hydrologic modeling of flood responses and allows for interfacing with the coastal hazards modeling process. The categories used for this approach are intentionally broad since the distinction is important for two uses: storm seasonality and isolating tropical storms. The seasonality of the storm events is used to determine initial watershed conditions during the stochastic simulations so that implausible combinations of meteorology and watershed state cannot be created. Isolation of tropical cyclones is important for handling additional coastal modeling that may be required to characterize compound hazards caused by the combination of rainfall and storm surge. Currently, for FFRD studies, two broad storm categories are used:

- Tropical Cyclones (TC)

- Non-Tropical (NT)

Most rainfall events that occur on a sufficiently widespread scale for inclusion in a HUC-4 scale watershed catalog will fall into the NT category.

Manual storm classification of each storm in the catalog is recommended for FFRD studies, as no cloud-compute software action for automated classification has been fully released. Recommended procedures for automated techniques and tools for storm classification are expected to be incorporated into a future version of the FFRD SOP.

### Manual Storm Classification

TC, which include all phases of the lifecycle of a tropical storm (e.g. tropical depression, hurricane, etc.), are most prevalent near the Gulf of Mexico and the Atlantic Coast, although Pacific storms can occur as well. Watersheds that are significantly inland may occasionally be impacted by remnants of tropical storms. Of all storm types, these are typically the easiest to identify due to the availability of datasets like IBTrACS or Hurricane Database (HURDAT), and their clear impact on surface analysis charts.

A combination of visual information is recommended for the classification process. Daily Weather Maps or other sources of surface analyses can often be used to rapidly identify Mid latitude cyclones and TCs. IBTrACS has a spatial product that makes visualization of TC tracks straightforward. Reanalysis datasets such as the contiguous United States for 40 years at 4-km resolution (CONUS404) produce useful visualizations of other key data indicators in space and can be interacted with using Python or other data visualization tools. Some key variables to evaluate include:

- Storm season

- Precipitation

- Convective Available Potential Energy (CAPE)

- Mean Sea-Level Pressure (MSLP)

- Storm Relative Helicity (SRH)

- 500 hPa geopotential height and gradient

Other data and visualizations used at the discretion of the analyst can assist in completing the classifications.

### Storm Seasonality Output

For each type of storm, a seasonality analysis will be produced. The seasonality analysis will describe the daily frequency of occurrence for each storm type based on the start date of the events in the storm catalog. Each day of the year from 01 January through 31 December will have a count of storm occurrence computed as the count of storms of that type beginning on that day of the year. The sum of the counts should equal the total number of storms of that type, and the sum of all tables should total the number of storms in the catalog.

Uniform placements will be applied for studies executed with version 1 of the SOP. ## Stochastic Meteorlogic Model Results

Basin-average precipitation frequency data from stochastic storm transposition must be extracted from modeled storm placements to evaluate the conformance of results to observed data.

SST is driven by the Uncertainty Analysis, randomly varying the storm selection and storm coordinates, and extracting the precipitation that results from each transposition. An Uncertainty Analysis occurs in HEC-HMS with three parameters: Storm Center X-coordinate, Storm Center Y coordinate, and grid name (representing an event from the storm catalog). Set up a precipitation only discretization basin model for the project with a single subbasin of the entire study domain and follow the steps presented in this [Precipitation-only SST Runs Guide](https://ttps/www.hec.usace.army.mil/confluence/spaces/WG/pages/166101670/Duwamish+River+Precipitation-Only+SST+Runs), and using [this procedure](https://www.hec.usace.army.mil/confluence/spaces/WG/pages/294954861/Generating+Subbasin+Averaged+Precipitation+AMS+With+HEC-HMS) to extract subbasin averaged annual maximum series.

The annual maximum series data of subbasin averaged precipitation must be posted, where it can be programmatically leveraged for analysis, plotting, and evaluation.
